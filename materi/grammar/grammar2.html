Grammar 4: Present Perfect Tense in English

The **Present Perfect Tense** is used to describe actions that happened at an **unspecified time** in the past or have **a connection to the present**. It is formed using **have/has + past participle** of the verb. Example: "She **has visited** Paris." / "They **have finished** their homework." We use **"just"**, **"already"**, and **"yet"** to indicate time. Example: "I **have just eaten** lunch." / "Has she finished her work **yet**?" The negative form is made using **haven't/hasn't** + past participle. Example: "He **hasn't seen** that movie."

Grammar 5: Present Perfect Continuous Tense in English

The **Present Perfect Continuous Tense** is used to describe an action that started in the past and is **still continuing** or **has recently stopped**. It is formed using **have/has + been + verb-ing**. Example: "She **has been studying** for three hours." / "They **have been working** since morning." We often use **"for"** (duration) and **"since"** (starting point) in this tense. Example: "He **has been living** here **since** 2010." The negative form is made using **haven't/hasn't been + verb-ing**. Example: "She **hasn't been sleeping** well lately."

Grammar 6: Past Tense in English

The **Past Tense** is used to describe actions that happened **in the past**. The **Simple Past Tense** is formed by adding **-ed** to regular verbs or using **irregular verb forms**. Example: "She **visited** her grandmother last weekend." / "They **went** to the park yesterday." The **Past Continuous Tense** is used for actions that were happening at a specific time in the past. It is formed using **was/were + verb-ing**. Example: "I **was watching** TV when you called." The negative form uses **did not (didn't) + base verb**. Example: "He **did not eat** breakfast this morning."
